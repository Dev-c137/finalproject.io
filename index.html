Our Process

Our team, G5 innovation, investigated Sony digital cameras, DSLR and Mirrorless, and what opportunities may exist to improve their overall experience by turning a smartphone or tablet into an extension of the camera. ​ Our team’s belief is that Sony digital cameras are a quality hardware product, but they require an improved experience to enhance and extend the capabilities of the camera, to coach newer users, and to maximize productivity for professionals who rely on Sony cameras to make art and run a business.​

Preliminary research

Some high-level research shows that digital cameras have lost significant ground to smartphones in the overall “taking pictures” market. Over 3 billion photos are shared on the internet each day, and over 90% of photos are taken with smartphones. 

In addition to always being “at hand”, an advantage that smartphones have are app stores, with developers coming up with new ways to utilize cameras in applications or creating enjoyable photo taking experiences.

So we wondered what might be possible if a Sony camera became hardware component of your smartphone. How could the smartphone extend and unlock the potential experience for all Sony camera users, from beginner to expert?​

Brainstorming

We cast a wide net in our initial brainstorming exercises, looking for the many the pain points from the user journeys we mapped, as well as our own experience using digital cameras.​For each of the problems we identified, we came up with multiple concepts for how to resolve them, and looked to find key themes and the best areas for impact.

We recognized there would likely be innovations more suitable to beginners, and some useful only for professionals, while some improvements could benefit all users.​ With that said… who should we be designing these improved experiences for? And how should we do it?

Personas

Our process involved identification of common user personas. We selected two personas at either end of the user skill spectrum and mapped their current experience to identify gaps and pain points that could be addressed.

Our first persona is Tricia, an entry-level DSLR user who is just embarking on a photography career. And our second persona is Alicia, a photography business owner who is constantly looking for ways to improve her craft and drive efficiency.

Journey Map

We took each of these personas and mapped a typical user journey for them, helping to identify common pain points or where there may be opportunities to innovate.

Our first user journey is for Tricia. It follows the story of her initial venture into photography, first with investment in an expensive new Sony DSLR camera. Her experience leads her to realize that the camera is a complex device and requires more work than just having a nice camera. With photos overexposed or poorly lit, and more settings and features than she can wrap her head around, Tricia spends the next several months putting time and money into tutorials and courses to improve her photography fundamentals and learn her camera. In the end, Tricia still feels like she’s not getting everything out of her camera, and that her return on initial investment leaves much to be desired.

Our second user journey is with Alicia who runs a photography business with multiple employees. Business is booming and she decides to hire another employee. With bookings coming in all the time, the team often has to split up leaving less time for hands on training and more room for user error or miscommunication. Photos from important clients end up with color inconsistencies and bad lighting, leading to hours at the computer fixing them. In the end Alicia gets paid and the client loves their photos, but she wonders what she could do to prevent these mistakes when the team is split up in the field.

Targeted Improvement Areas

We eventually distilled our brainstorming into three targeted areas of improvement: the camera learning experience, the interface and settings, and networking of multiple camera devices at once.
